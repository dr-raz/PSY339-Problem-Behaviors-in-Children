3  Assessment

Take a Moment: What Do You Think Assessment Is?

Before reading, pause and think about how clinicians decide whether a child meets criteria for a diagnosis. When does something go from a quirk or a particular trait to a disorder and how do we measure it? Do you imagine a single test, a checklist, or one interview providing a clear answer? What do you think are the most important things to ask and assess?

3.1 What Is Psychological Assessment?

Psychological assessment refers to the methods, procedures, and tools used to collect, organize, and interpret information about a child in order to identify and understand difficulties and guide intervention. Assessment is not a single event or test. Instead, it is an ongoing process that involves gathering evidence, developing hypotheses about what may be maintaining a child’s problems, and revising those hypotheses as new information becomes available. This process of integrating information and forming explanations is often referred to as case conceptualization.

Assessment in child psychology is multi-method and multi-source. Clinicians intentionally collect information using different tools and from multiple informants, including children, caregivers, teachers, and other professionals. Assessment is also theoretically informed and empirically supported, meaning that clinicians rely on established psychological theories and research-based tools while remaining sensitive to the child’s developmental level and context. Importantly, assessment is collaborative and requires rapport, developmental sensitivity, and the child’s active participation.

3.2 What Does Assessment Look Like in Children?

Assessment in children focuses on patterns of thoughts, emotions, and behaviors across time and settings. Because children’s functioning is strongly shaped by their environment, clinicians avoid relying on a single measure or perspective. Instead, assessment combines several core methods, each offering different but complementary information. Unlike adult assessment, because a child may not be able to articulate they symptoms, child assessment may rely heavily on information from people around the child, the most common being caregivers and teachers.

3.2.1 Clinical interviews

Clinical interviews are structured or semi-structured conversations designed to gather a large amount of information efficiently. Interviews are often conducted with caregivers, children, and sometimes teachers or other professionals. A major focus is obtaining a detailed history.

Information commonly gathered includes:

  • Presenting problem and reason for referral

  • Developmental history, including early milestones

  • Medical history

  • Family and social history

  • Educational history

Example questions:

  • What concerns led you to seek help at this time?

  • When did these behaviors first begin?

  • In what settings do the difficulties appear most often?

Example clinical interviews:

  • DIAMOND-KID Diagnostic Interview

  • Child Diagnostic Interview Protocol

3.2.2 Behavioral observations

Behavioral observation involves directly watching a child’s behavior rather than relying solely on reports. Observations may be structured, such as during testing tasks, or informal and naturalistic, such as in classrooms or play settings. Observations are considered crucial because they provide real-time information about behavior.

Clinicians often focus on the ABCs of behavior:

  • Antecedents: what happens before the behavior

  • Behaviors: what the child does

  • Consequences: what happens after the behavior

3.2.3 Standardized Assessment Methods

Standardized assessments are administered and scored in a consistent way so that results can be interpreted relative to either norms or criteria. These measures assess a range of domains relevant to child functioning.

Norm-Referenced and Criterion-Referenced Measures

Assessment tools differ in how results are interpreted.Norm-referenced measures compare a child’s performance to that of a peer group.

Example:

  • A child’s score on the Child Behavior Checklist (CBCL) is compared to national norms to determine whether symptoms fall in the typical or clinical range.

Criterion-referenced measures compare performance to a fixed standard or set of skills. These are also considered achievement tests. Has the child “achieved” this certain level of a particular task or subject.

Example:

  • A clinician evaluates whether a child can independently perform specific academic (writing name on paper, solving addition problems, etc.) or adaptive tasks (tying shoes, feeding self).

3.2.4 Rating Scales and Checklists

Rating scales and checklists are standardized questionnaires completed by parents, teachers, or older children. These tools are economical to administer and score and allow behavior to be compared to known reference groups. Lack of agreement between informants is common and can be clinically meaningful, often reflecting differences across settings.

Examples of commonly used rating scales:

  • Child Behavior Checklist (CBCL)

  • The Child Symptom Inventory–4

  • NICHQ Vanderbilt Assessment Scales for ADHD

  • Eyberg Child Behavior Inventory

  • Beck Depression Inventory (BDI) or Children’s Depression Inventory (CDI)

3.2.5 Intelligence tests

Intelligence tests assess reasoning, problem-solving, and information processing abilities. These tests are norm-referenced and compare a child’s performance to that of same-age peers.

Examples include:

  • Wechsler Intelligence Scale for Children (WISC)

  • Wechsler Preschool and Primary Scale of Intelligence (WPPSI)

  • Kaufman Assessment Battery for Children

3.2.6 Achievement tests

Achievement tests evaluate academic skills such as reading, writing, and math. Has the child achieved a certain level that would be expected.

Examples include:

  • Wide Range Achievement Test (WRAT)

  • Peabody Picture Vocabulary Test (PPVT)

  • Woodcock-Johnson Tests of Achievement

3.2.7 Developmental and adaptive behavior assessments

Developmental assessments focus on early developmental milestones, while adaptive behavior scales assess daily living skills and independence.

Examples include:

  • Denver Developmental Screening Test

  • Vineland Adaptive Behavior Scales

3.2.8 Neuropsychological assessment

Neuropsychological assessment attempts to link brain functioning with behavior. These assessments use comprehensive batteries to evaluate attention, memory, executive functioning, language, and other cognitive processes that depend on the central nervous system.

3.2.9 Projective and expressive techniques

Projective techniques are sometimes used to explore emotional functioning and internal experiences. These methods are interpreted cautiously and typically supplement, rather than replace, other assessment tools.

Examples include:

  • Children’s Thematic Apperception Test (C-TAT)

  • Sentence completion tasks such as the Rotter Incomplete Sentences Blank

  • Drawings, such as family drawings

3.3 Assessment, Diagnosis, and Treatment

Assessment, diagnosis, and treatment are interconnected components of the helping process. Assessment guides treatment planning by identifying patterns and testing hypotheses about what maintains a child’s difficulties. Diagnosis labels patterns of experience in order to connect the child to an existing knowledge base, but diagnoses describe problems rather than defining the child. Treatment then targets identified concerns, and assessment continues throughout treatment to monitor progress and adjust interventions.

3.4 What Assessment Can and Cannot Do

Assessment can clarify strengths and challenges, inform diagnosis, and guide intervention planning. Assessment cannot provide certainty, predict outcomes perfectly, or capture every aspect of a child’s functioning. Diagnoses should be understood as informed judgments based on available evidence rather than absolute truths. As an aside, a person is more than their diagnosis and we should never reduce a person down to a label. Diagnosis are meant to guide intervention and treatment. Determining a diagnosis is usually necessary in order to receive services. For example, most insurance companies require a diagnosis to pay for treatment. While identifying and determining a diagnosis is important, it is more important to keep a holistic view of the person and provide services that improve their quality of life.

3.6 Key Terms

Assessment: The systematic process of collecting and integrating information to understand functioning and guide intervention.

Case conceptualization: The process of integrating assessment information to generate hypotheses about a child’s difficulties.

Clinical interview: A structured or semi-structured conversation used to gather developmental, symptom, and contextual information.

Criterion-referenced measure: An assessment that evaluates performance against a fixed standard or skill set, ex. achievement tests

Neuropsychological assessment: A comprehensive evaluation linking brain functioning to behavior.

Norm-referenced measure: An assessment that compares an individual’s performance to a peer group, ex. IQ tests

Rating scale: A standardized questionnaire used to assess emotional or behavioral symptoms.

Standardized assessment: A test administered and scored using consistent procedures that allow comparison across individuals.
