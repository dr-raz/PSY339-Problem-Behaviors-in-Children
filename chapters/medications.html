7  Medications in Children

Take a Moment: What Do You Think About Prescribing Medication to Children?

Before reading this chapter, take a moment to think about the role medication plays in treating psychological and behavioral difficulties in children and adolescents. When do you think medication might be helpful for a child? What do you think medication can realistically change?

7.1 What Is Psychopharmacology in Children?

Psychopharmacology refers to the use of medication to help manage psychological, emotional, or behavioral symptoms. In children, this process requires special care because their brains and bodies are still developing.

Prescribing medication for children differs from prescribing for adults in several important ways. Children often require lower doses, careful titration, and more frequent monitoring. A medication that is helpful at one developmental stage may not be appropriate at another. Because of this, providers regularly reassess whether a medication is still necessary and effective.

7.2 Why Are Medications Prescribed for Children?

Medications are prescribed to target specific symptoms rather than to cure a disorder. The goal is often to reduce the intensity or frequency of symptoms that interfere with daily functioning.

Common reasons medications may be prescribed include:

  • Improving attention, impulse control, or emotional regulation

  • Reducing anxiety or persistent low mood

  • Decreasing severe behavioral outbursts or aggression

  • Helping a child better engage in therapy or school activities

When effective, medication can make it easier for children to learn new skills, participate in therapy, and function more successfully at home and school.

7.3 Major Categories of Medications Used in Children

This section provides a high-level overview of common categories of medications prescribed to children. It is not meant to be exhaustive or prescriptive.

7.3.1 Stimulants

Stimulant medications are most commonly prescribed for attention and impulse-control difficulties. These medications often help children sustain attention, reduce hyperactivity, and improve self-control.

7.3.2 Non-Stimulant ADHD Medications

Non-stimulant medications may be used when stimulants are not effective or are not well tolerated. These medications also target attention and impulse control but work through different mechanisms. They are sometimes chosen for children who experience significant side effects from stimulants or who have co-occurring conditions that make stimulant use less appropriate.

7.3.3 Antidepressants

Antidepressant medications are often prescribed to children and adolescents experiencing anxiety or mood-related symptoms. These medications may help reduce persistent worry, sadness, or irritability.

7.3.4 Antianxiety Medications

Some medications are prescribed specifically to reduce acute anxiety symptoms. These are often used cautiously in children and may be prescribed for short-term or situational use. Because of potential side effects and dependency concerns, careful monitoring is especially important when these medications are used with younger populations.

7.3.5 Antipsychotic Medications

Antipsychotic medications may be prescribed to children experiencing severe emotional or behavioral dysregulation, such as intense aggression or extreme mood instability. These medications are typically reserved for situations in which symptoms pose significant safety or functional concerns. Providers carefully weigh potential benefits against known risks.

7.3.6 Mood Stabilizers

Mood stabilizers are sometimes used to help regulate intense mood swings or emotional instability. While less commonly prescribed in children, they may be considered in specific clinical situations. As with other medications, close monitoring and regular follow-up are essential.

7.4 Benefits and Limitations of Medication

Medication can offer meaningful benefits, but it also has clear limitations.

Potential benefits include:

  • Reduction in symptom severity

  • Improved participation in school or therapy

  • Increased emotional or behavioral stability

However, medication does not teach coping skills, social skills, or problem-solving strategies. It also does not address environmental factors that contribute to a child’s difficulties. Because of this, medication is most effective when combined with therapy, behavioral interventions, and supportive environments.

7.5 Common Side Effects and Monitoring

All medications carry the risk of side effects. In children, common concerns may include changes in sleep, appetite, energy levels, or mood.

Ongoing monitoring is critical. This often involves:

  • Regular check-ins with caregivers and the child

  • Communication with teachers or school staff

  • Adjusting dosage or discontinuing medication if needed

The goal is always to balance symptom reduction with overall well-being.

7.6 Ethical and Practical Considerations

Several ethical and practical issues are important when prescribing medication to children.

  • Children cannot provide full informed consent, so caregivers play a central role in decision-making

  • Children should still be included in age-appropriate discussions about their treatment

  • Stigma surrounding medication use can influence family decisions

Clinicians must also be cautious not to rely solely on medication when other effective interventions are available.

7.7 Medications in Context

Medication is best understood as one part of a comprehensive treatment plan. For many children, medication helps reduce barriers so that other interventions can work more effectively. For example, reducing severe inattention may allow a child to benefit more fully from cognitive-behavioral therapy, classroom instruction, or behavioral interventions. Medication supports change, but it rarely produces lasting change on its own.

7.8 Reflection and Discussion Questions

  1. When might medication be an appropriate part of treatment for a child?

  2. What concerns do caregivers often have about medication use?

  3. How should clinicians balance symptom reduction with potential side effects?

  4. What are some of the problems and limitations of using medication alone, without any additional therapy or support?

7.9 Key Terms

  • Antidepressants
    Medications commonly prescribed to reduce symptoms of anxiety and depression, such as persistent sadness, worry, or irritability, by altering levels of certain neurotransmitters in the brain.

  • Antianxiety Medications
    Medications used to reduce symptoms of anxiety, such as intense fear, physiological arousal, or panic. These are often prescribed cautiously and may be used short-term in children.

  • Antipsychotic Medications
    Medications sometimes prescribed to children to manage severe emotional dysregulation, aggression, or psychotic-like symptoms. These medications require careful monitoring due to potential side effects.

  • Monitoring
    Ongoing assessment of a child’s response to medication, including symptom changes, side effects, and overall functioning across settings such as home and school.

  • Mood Stabilizers
    Medications used to help regulate significant mood swings or emotional instability. These are less commonly prescribed in children but may be used in specific clinical circumstances.

  • Non-Stimulant ADHD Medications
    Medications used to treat attention and impulse-control difficulties that do not fall within the stimulant class. These may be used when stimulants are ineffective or poorly tolerated.

  • Psychopharmacology
    The study and use of medications to manage psychological, emotional, or behavioral symptoms.

  • Side Effects
    Unintended physical, emotional, or behavioral changes that may occur as a result of taking a medication.

  • Stimulants
    A class of medications most commonly prescribed for attention and impulse-control difficulties, often improving focus, activity level, and self-regulation.

  • Symptom Management
    The goal of reducing the severity or frequency of specific symptoms to improve daily functioning, rather than eliminating the underlying disorder.
