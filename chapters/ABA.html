5  Applied Behavior Analysis

Take a Moment: What Do You Think Applied Behavior Analysis is?

This course is titled Problem Behaviors in Children. Before reading this chapter, pause and think about the following questions:

  • What causes/creates problem behavior in children?

  • What maintains problem behavior over time?

  • How do children learn new behaviors, especially when learning does not occur automatically?

5.1 What Is Applied Behavior Analysis?

Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA) is the systematic application of principles of learning to improve socially significant behaviors and to demonstrate that the intervention itself is responsible for the observed change. In simple terms, ABA uses what we know about how behavior works to help people learn new skills and reduce behaviors that interfere with daily functioning.

ABA grew out of behaviorism, a theoretical perspective that focuses on observable behavior rather than internal mental states. Early behaviorists, such as B.F. Skinner, were interested in how behavior is shaped by the environment, particularly by the consequences that follow actions. By the 1960s, these ideas began to be applied outside the laboratory, leading to the development of ABA as an applied science.

Today, ABA is most commonly associated with intervention for Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD), but its principles can also be used in many other diagnoses, educational settings, behavior management, addiction treatment, organizational settings, sports, and everyday behavior change.

5.2 Learning Foundations of ABA

5.2.1 Classical Conditioning

Classical conditioning involves learning through association. One stimulus comes to signal that another stimulus is about to occur. This type of learning is reflexive and does not depend on what the individual does.

Example 1: Exam anxiety
A student repeatedly studies late at night while feeling stressed and exhausted before major exams. Over time, simply opening the course website or seeing the exam room can trigger anxiety.

  • Unconditioned stimulus (US): the high-stakes exam

  • Unconditioned response (UR): anxiety

  • Neutral stimulus (NS): course website or exam room

  • Conditioned stimulus (CS): course website or exam room

  • Conditioned response (CR): anxiety

Example 2: Notification sounds
A student hears their phone notification sound repeatedly just before receiving messages from friends. Eventually, the notification sound alone produces excitement or anticipation.

  • Unconditioned stimulus (US): messages from friends

  • Unconditioned response (UR): excitement

  • Neutral stimulus (NS): notification sound

  • Conditioned stimulus (CS): notification sound

  • Conditioned response (CR): excitement

Although classical conditioning plays a role in some behaviors addressed in ABA, such as fear responses or emotional reactions, it is not the primary learning mechanism used in most ABA interventions.

5.2.2 Operant Conditioning

ABA is primarily grounded in operant conditioning. Operant conditioning focuses on how behavior is shaped and maintained by its consequences. Behaviors that are followed by reinforcing consequences are more likely to occur again, while behaviors followed by punishing consequences are less likely to recur.

Example 1: Cheating and getting caught
A student cheats on an exam and is caught by the instructor.

  • Antecedent (Discriminative Stimuli): other exams and a big party this weekend

  • Behavior (Response): cheating on the exam

  • Consequence (Stimuli Punishment+): being forced to give an embarrassing public apology, receiving a severe reprimand, and getting a zero

  • Effect on behavior: the likelihood of cheating decreases in the future

Example 2: Cheating and not getting caught
A student cheats on an exam and is not caught.

  • Antecedent (Discriminative Stimuli ): other exams and a big party this weekend

  • Behavior (Response): cheating on the exam

  • Consequence (Stimuli Reinforcement+): receiving a high grade and praise

  • Effect on behavior: the likelihood of cheating increases in the future

From an operant perspective, learning is not just about what happens to someone, but about what someone does and what happens afterward. This focus on behavior–consequence relationships is central to ABA.

5.3 The ABC Model of Behavior

A core framework in ABA is the ABC model, which breaks behavior into three components.

5.3.1 Antecedent

The antecedent (Latin: “ante”-before, + “cedere”-go = “going before”) is what happens before the behavior. This includes the environment, who is present, what instructions are given, the time of day, the person’s emotional state at that moment, and other contextual factors. Antecedents help set the stage for behavior and can often be modified to prevent problem behaviors or encourage appropriate ones.

5.3.2 Behavior

The behavior is the observable and measurable action of interest. In ABA, behaviors must be defined in clear, objective terms so that different observers would agree on whether the behavior occurred.

5.3.3 Consequence

The consequence is what happens after the behavior. Consequences influence whether the behavior is likely to happen again. They may involve reinforcement, punishment, or no change at all.

Example: A child with low blood sugar sees and wants a candy at the checkout line in the grocery store. The child starts begging and whining for the candy. The frustrated parent does not want to hear the crying so they give the candy bar to child so they will stop whining.

Antecedence: Child is hungry (low blood sugar)

Behavior: Begging and whining for candy

Consequence: Parent gives child candy

Child has learned that whining will get them what they want. The parents also learns that giving the child what they want will make them stop whining… 

5.4 Reinforcement and Punishment

Before defining reinforcement and punishment, it is important to clarify what positive and negative mean in ABA. In this context, positive and negative do not mean good or bad. Instead:

  • Positive means adding something to the environment.

  • Negative means removing something from the environment.

Whether something is reinforcing or punishing depends entirely on its effect on future behavior, not on whether it seems pleasant or unpleasant to an observer.

5.4.1 Reinforcement

Reinforcement increases the likelihood that a behavior will occur again.

  • Positive reinforcement involves adding something desirable after a behavior.

    • Example: Giving a child a treat or a “Good job!” for good behavior. 

  • Negative reinforcement involves removing something aversive after a behavior.

    • Example: Because the child was helpful to their sibling, they are allowed to not do one of their chores. 

    • Example: Because you turned in all your assignments and did well on each benchmark, you can skip the last one. 

Reinforcers can be primary, such as food or physical comfort, or secondary, such as praise, attention, or tokens. Reinforcers also vary widely across individuals, which is why identifying what is motivating for a specific person is essential. 

5.4.2 Punishment

Punishment decreases the likelihood that a behavior will occur again.

  • Positive punishment involves adding an aversive consequence.

    • Example: Corporal punishment (which should NEVER be used)

    • Example: Assigning a chore/task 

  • Negative punishment involves removing something desirable.

    • Example: Taking away a desirable item like an iPad 

    • Example: Grounding a child (i.e., removing social interaction)

While punishment can reduce behavior, it carries risks, including emotional side effects and failure to teach appropriate alternatives. For this reason, modern ABA emphasizes reinforcement and skill-building over punishment.

5.5 Understanding the Function of Behavior

In ABA, behaviors are understood in terms of their function, or purpose. The function explains why a behavior is occurring and what is maintaining it.

Four broad functions of behavior are commonly identified:

  • Social positive reinforcement, such as gaining attention or access to preferred items

  • Social negative reinforcement, such as escaping demands or unpleasant situations

  • Automatic positive reinforcement, where the behavior itself is enjoyable

  • Automatic negative reinforcement, where the behavior reduces discomfort

Identifying the function of a behavior is critical. The same behavior may serve different functions for different individuals, and effective intervention depends on addressing the correct function.

5.6 Functional Behavior Assessment (FBA)

A Functional Behavior Assessment is a systematic process used to identify the antecedents, consequences, and likely function of a behavior.

There are several approaches to conducting an FBA:

  • Indirect assessments rely on interviews and reports from caregivers or teachers.

  • Descriptive assessments involve direct observation and collection of ABC data.

  • Experimental assessments involve systematically manipulating antecedents or consequences, though these are less common in many applied settings.

The goal of an FBA is not simply to label behavior, but to guide effective and ethical intervention.

5.7 Behavior Intervention Plans (BIPs)

A Behavior Intervention Plan is a written document that outlines how a specific behavior will be addressed.

A typical BIP includes:

  • An operational definition of the target behavior

  • The suspected function of the behavior

  • Antecedent modifications

  • Replacement behaviors to teach

  • Consequence strategies

  • A plan for measurement and data collection

Effective BIPs focus on teaching appropriate replacement behaviors that serve the same function as the problem behavior, rather than simply suppressing behavior.

5.8 Teaching New Skills in ABA

5.8.1 Discrete Trial Training (DTT)

Discrete Trial Training is a structured teaching method commonly used in ABA. Each trial has a clear beginning and end and follows the ABC sequence.

A typical trial includes:

  • A discriminative stimulus, such as an instruction

  • A response by the learner

  • A consequence, such as reinforcement or correction

Responses are recorded as correct, incorrect, no response, or prompted. A prompt is additional assistance given to help the learner produce the correct response, such as a verbal hint, gesture, or physical guidance. Prompts are used to support learning and are gradually faded over time so the learner can respond independently. This structure allows for consistent teaching and precise data collection. 

5.8.2 Programs, Targets, and Mastery

Skills are broken down into smaller components called targets. Targets are taught one at a time, and mastery is determined based on consistent performance across multiple trials.

ABA programs often distinguish between receptive skills, which involve understanding and responding to instructions, and expressive skills, which involve producing responses such as speech or gestures.

Receptive: Typically involve some sort of stimuli, like cards with images on them.

  • “Give me ______.”

  • “Show me _____.”

  • “Touch _______.”

Expressive: Involves a response that is either verbal (or some sort of augmented communication method)

  • “What is this called?”

  • “Who is this person”

  • “What do you do with this?”

5.9 Measuring and Tracking Behavior

Data collection is a defining feature of ABA. Without data, it is impossible to know whether an intervention is effective.

Two broad types of data are commonly used:

  • Continuous data, such as frequency, duration, and latency, are used for behaviors that occur throughout a session.

  • Trial-by-trial data are used when teaching new skills.

Raw data are often summarized using rates, percentages, averages, and graphs. Visual analysis allows practitioners to quickly identify trends, improvements, or concerns. Reliability across observers is also important to ensure data accuracy.

5.10 Schedules of Reinforcement

Reinforcement does not always occur after every response.

  • Continuous reinforcement involves reinforcing every correct response and is typically used when teaching new behaviors.

  • Intermittent reinforcement involves reinforcing some, but not all, responses and more closely resembles real-life conditions.

Intermittent schedules can be based on ratios or intervals and can be fixed or variable. Different schedules produce different patterns of responding and affect how resistant a behavior is to extinction.

5.11 Ethical Considerations and Critiques

ABA has been criticized for overemphasizing compliance and control, particularly in its early history. Concerns include the risk of reducing autonomy, promoting learned helplessness, and prioritizing adult convenience over individual needs. Modern ABA emphasizes flexibility, functional communication, and respect for the individual. Ethical practice involves continually asking whether interventions are helping the person meaningfully and whether goals align with quality of life rather than mere obedience.

5.12 ABA Beyond ASD

Official ABA, particularly the kind that is covered by insurance, is most commonly provided for children with Autism Spectrum Disorder. Insurance-funded ABA services require a formal diagnosis, oversight by a licensed or board-certified provider, clearly defined target behaviors, measurable goals, and extensive data collection to demonstrate progress. Treatment plans must be justified through functional behavior assessments, written behavior intervention plans, and regular documentation showing that services are effective and medically necessary. This level of structure and accountability is one reason ABA is recognized as an evidence-based intervention and is eligible for insurance reimbursement.

However, the core principles of ABA are not limited to autism. The same learning principles that explain and change behavior in ASD also apply to many other disorders and everyday problem behaviors. Children with anxiety, ADHD, disruptive behavior disorders, or emotion regulation difficulties may engage in behaviors that are unintentionally created or maintained by their environments. In many cases, well-meaning parents and caregivers reinforce problem behaviors without realizing it, such as giving in to tantrums to stop distress or inconsistently following through with consequences. ABA provides a framework for understanding how consequences, consistency, and learning history shape behavior, and these principles can be used alongside other treatments, such as cognitive-behavioral therapy, medication, or family-based interventions, to support meaningful behavior change across a wide range of contexts.

5.14 Key Terms

  • Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA): A scientific approach that applies principles of learning to improve socially significant behavior and uses data to demonstrate that interventions are responsible for behavior change.

  • Antecedent: Any event, condition, or stimulus that occurs before a behavior and sets the occasion for that behavior to occur.

  • Behavior: An observable and measurable action performed by an individual.

  • Consequence: An event that occurs after a behavior and influences the likelihood that the behavior will occur again.

  • Reinforcement: A consequence that increases the future likelihood of a behavior.

  • Punishment: A consequence that decreases the future likelihood of a behavior.

  • Discriminative Stimulus (SD): A stimulus that signals that a particular behavior is likely to be reinforced or punished if it occurs.

  • Functional Behavior Assessment (FBA): A systematic process used to identify the antecedents, consequences, and function of a behavior.

  • Behavior Intervention Plan (BIP): A written plan that outlines strategies to reduce problem behavior and teach appropriate replacement behaviors based on the results of an FBA.

  • Discrete Trial Training (DTT): A structured teaching method in ABA that breaks skills into small, teachable units with clear antecedents, responses, and consequences.

  • Schedules of Reinforcement: Rules that specify when and how often reinforcement is delivered following a behavior.
