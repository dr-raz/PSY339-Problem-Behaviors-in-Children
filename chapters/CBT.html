6  Cognitive Behavioral Therapy

Take a Moment: What Do You Think Cognitive Behavior Therapy is?

Think about the last time you felt very anxious, sad, or frustrated. Before anything actually happened, what went through your mind? Did you tell yourself something about the situation, about other people, or about yourself? Now think about how that thought influenced how you felt and what you did next.

  • Example: A middle school student who sees their name on the board for talking in class and immediately thinks, “I’m always in trouble.” That thought may lead to embarrassment or anger, followed by shutting down or acting out.

CBT begins with the idea that our thoughts, emotions, and behaviors are closely connected. By learning to notice and change patterns in one area, we can influence the others.

6.1 What Is Cognitive Behavioral Therapy?

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT) is a structured, evidence-based psychological treatment that focuses on the relationships between thoughts, emotions, and behaviors. The core assumption of CBT is that psychological distress is often maintained not just by situations themselves, but by how people interpret those situations and how they respond to them.

Rather than focusing heavily on early childhood experiences or unconscious processes, CBT emphasizes present-day functioning. The goal is to help individuals develop practical skills they can use to change unhelpful thinking patterns, manage emotional responses, and engage in more adaptive behaviors.

  • Example: Instead of exploring why a child has always been shy, CBT would focus on what the child thinks when asked to speak in class, how that thought affects their anxiety, and what they do next.

CBT is collaborative, goal-oriented, and skills-based. Clients are active participants in treatment rather than passive recipients.

6.2 A Brief History of CBT

CBT developed from two major traditions in psychology: behavioral therapy and cognitive therapy. Behavioral therapy emerged from learning theory and focused on how behaviors are acquired and maintained through reinforcement and punishment. Cognitive therapy later emphasized the role of thoughts and beliefs in emotional distress. Over time, these approaches were integrated into what is now known as CBT. This integration represented a shift away from viewing behavior as the only meaningful target of intervention, toward recognizing that thoughts and interpretations play a central role in shaping emotional and behavioral responses.

6.3 The CBT Model

At the core of CBT is the idea that thoughts, emotions, and behaviors influence one another. Thoughts refer to interpretations, beliefs, or mental statements about a situation. Emotions are the feelings that arise in response to those thoughts. Behaviors are the actions taken as a result of both thoughts and emotions.

Situation: A student is called on in class and gives an incorrect answer.

Thoughts (what we tell ourselves):

  • “I sound stupid. Everyone thinks I don’t belong in this class.”

Emotions (how we feel in response):

  • Embarrassment, anxiety, and shame.

Behaviors (how we act based on those thoughts and emotions):

  • Avoids raising their hand in the future, looks down, withdraws from class participation.

A change in any one of these components can lead to changes in the others. Changing the thought to, “I might be nervous, but I can still get through this,” may reduce anxiety and increase the likelihood of attending class. This relationship is often illustrated using the CBT triangle, which highlights the ongoing interaction between thoughts, feelings, and behaviors.

6.4 The CBT Triangle: Thoughts, Emotions, and Behaviors

After understanding the basic CBT model, treatment focuses heavily on the CBT triangle. The triangle illustrates how thoughts, emotions, and behaviors continuously influence one another in everyday situations.

A situation itself does not directly cause emotional distress. Instead, it is the interpretation of the situation that shapes emotional reactions and behavioral responses.

  • Example: Two adolescents are both asked to read aloud in class. One thinks, “I might mess up, but that’s okay,” feels mildly nervous, and participates. The other thinks, “Everyone will think I’m stupid,” feels intense anxiety, and asks to leave the room. The situation is the same, but the CBT triangle looks very different.

Understanding this triangle helps children and adolescents see that changing one part of the triangle can change the entire experience.

6.4.1 Thoughts

Thoughts are the interpretations, beliefs, or mental statements people make about situations. In CBT, special attention is paid to automatic thoughts because they occur quickly and often go unnoticed. Automatic thoughts feel true, even when they are inaccurate or extreme. Children and adolescents often treat these thoughts as facts rather than interpretations.

  • Example: A child who is picked last for a team might think, “No one ever wants me,” which can quickly trigger sadness or anger.

CBT teaches individuals to slow down and notice these thoughts, creating space to examine whether they are accurate, fair, or helpful.

Cognitive Distortions

Cognitive distortions are common patterns of thinking that are biased, exaggerated, or irrational. Everyone experiences cognitive distortions at times, especially under stress.

Examples include all-or-nothing thinking, catastrophizing, overgeneralization, and mind reading. These distortions can maintain anxiety, depression, and other forms of distress by reinforcing negative interpretations of events.

  • All-or-nothing thinking: seeing things in extreme terms with no middle ground (e.g., “I made one mistake, so I completely failed”)

  • Catastrophizing: assuming the worst possible outcome will happen (e.g., “If I mess up this presentation, I’ll lose my job”)

  • Overgeneralization: making a broad conclusion based on a single event (e.g., “I failed this test, so I’m bad at everything”)

  • Mind reading: assuming you know what others are thinking without evidence (e.g., “He didn’t say hi, so he must dislike me”)

Example: An adolescent who gets one poor quiz grade may think, “I’m going to fail everything,” even though this conclusion is not supported by the evidence.

CBT does not label distorted thoughts as bad or wrong. Instead, the goal is to help individuals recognize these patterns and evaluate whether they are accurate or helpful.

6.4.2 Emotions

Emotions are the feelings that arise in response to thoughts. Different thoughts about the same situation can produce very different emotional reactions.

  • Example: The thought “I’m going to fail this test” may lead to anxiety or panic, while the thought “This test will be hard, but I studied” may lead to manageable nervousness.

In CBT, emotions are not viewed as problems to eliminate. Instead, they are treated as signals that provide information about what someone is thinking. Helping children label and understand their emotions is an important step in changing the triangle.

6.4.3 Behaviors

Behaviors are the actions people take in response to their thoughts and emotions. These behaviors can either reduce distress in the short term or maintain problems over time. Avoidance is a common behavioral response in children and adolescents.

  • Example: A child who feels anxious about school may stay home, which reduces anxiety temporarily but increases fear of school in the long run.

Other behaviors, such as reassurance-seeking, withdrawal, or acting out, can also strengthen unhelpful patterns within the triangle. CBT helps individuals practice new behaviors that support long-term improvement, even when emotions are uncomfortable.

6.5 CBT Techniques and Tools

CBT uses a variety of structured tools to help individuals examine and change thought and behavior patterns. Thought monitoring involves identifying automatic thoughts in specific situations, such as noticing what a child thinks right before a tantrum. Cognitive restructuring focuses on evaluating and modifying unhelpful thoughts by asking questions like, “What is the evidence for this thought?” Behavioral experiments allow individuals to test predictions through real-world experience, such as seeing what actually happens when they raise their hand in class. These techniques emphasize practice and skill development rather than insight alone.

A key principle of CBT is that changing any one part of the triangle can influence the others. Changing thoughts might involve questioning a belief such as “I can’t handle this.” Changing behaviors might involve gradually facing feared situations rather than avoiding them. Changes in emotional responses often follow changes in thoughts and behaviors.

One way CBT targets behavior is through behavioral activation. Behavioral activation focuses on helping children and adolescents increase engagement in activities that are meaningful, enjoyable, or important, even when motivation is low. By changing what a child does, CBT can indirectly improve mood and reduce avoidance.

  • Example: A middle school student who feels depressed stops participating in after-school clubs and spends most afternoons alone in their room. Through behavioral activation, the therapist helps the student gradually reintroduce activities, such as attending one club meeting per week. As the student becomes more engaged, their mood improves and negative thoughts become less intense.

CBT also targets the triangle through cognitive restructuring, which focuses on changing unhelpful thoughts. Cognitive restructuring teaches children and adolescents to identify automatic thoughts, examine the evidence for those thoughts, and develop more balanced alternatives.

  • Example: A high school student who thinks, “If I make one mistake on this test, I’m going to fail the class,” learns to challenge this thought by looking at past grades and considering alternative explanations. Replacing the thought with, “One mistake won’t ruin everything,” helps reduce anxiety and supports more effective studying.

Together, behavioral activation and cognitive restructuring show how changing behavior and thoughts can reshape emotions and strengthen the entire CBT triangle.

6.6 Homework in CBT

Homework is a central component of CBT. Skills learned during sessions are practiced between sessions to promote real-world change. Homework assignments may include keeping track of thoughts, practicing coping skills, scheduling pleasant activities, or completing exposure steps.

  • Example: A child working on social anxiety might practice saying hello to one new peer each day.

Homework is collaborative and flexible. Assignments are adjusted based on what is realistic and helpful for the individual and their family.

6.7 What Does a CBT Session Look Like?

CBT sessions are typically structured and time-limited. Sessions often include setting an agenda, reviewing homework, discussing current concerns, practicing skills, and planning tasks for the coming week. With children and adolescents, sessions may include games, visual aids, or role-playing to help make skills more concrete and engaging.

6.8 What Is CBT Used For?

CBT is used to treat a wide range of psychological concerns, including anxiety disorders, depressive disorders, trauma-related disorders, obsessive-compulsive and related disorders, and problems with emotional regulation. CBT can also be helpful for behavior problems, stress management, and coping with chronic illness in children and adolescents.

6.9 CBT in Children and Adolescents

CBT can be adapted for children and adolescents by using developmentally appropriate language, visuals, and concrete examples. Treatment often involves caregivers, especially when working with younger children. Parents may help reinforce skills at home, support homework completion, and model adaptive thinking. Skills are framed as tools children can use at school, at home, and with peers.

6.10 CBT vs. ABA

CBT and Applied Behavior Analysis share an emphasis on structure, measurement, and skill development, but they differ in primary focus. CBT targets internal cognitive processes in addition to behavior, whereas ABA focuses primarily on observable behavior and environmental contingencies. Both approaches rely on consistent practice and data-informed decision making and are often used together in real-world settings.

6.11 Evidence Base and Insurance Coverage

CBT is one of the most extensively researched psychological treatments and is widely considered an evidence-based intervention. Because of its strong empirical support and structured nature, CBT is commonly covered by insurance providers, particularly for anxiety and mood disorders in youth.

6.12 Strengths and Limitations of CBT

CBT is effective, time-limited, and skills-focused. It helps children and adolescents develop tools they can continue using long after therapy ends. However, CBT may be less effective when individuals have difficulty identifying thoughts or are not ready to engage actively in treatment.

6.14 Key Terms

• Automatic Thoughts: Immediate, often unexamined thoughts that occur in response to a situation and strongly influence emotions and behavior. These thoughts feel true in the moment, even when they are inaccurate or exaggerated.

Behavioral Activation: A CBT strategy that focuses on increasing engagement in meaningful or enjoyable activities in order to improve mood, especially in individuals experiencing depression.

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy (CBT): An evidence-based psychological treatment that helps individuals understand and change unhelpful patterns of thinking and behavior in order to improve emotional well-being and functioning.

Cognitive Distortions: Common, biased patterns of thinking that are inaccurate or unhelpful, such as catastrophizing or all-or-nothing thinking, which can maintain emotional distress.

Cognitive Restructuring: A CBT technique that involves identifying, evaluating, and modifying unhelpful or distorted thoughts to develop more balanced and realistic ways of thinking.

Exposure: A behavioral technique in CBT that involves gradually and repeatedly facing feared situations, thoughts, or sensations in a planned and supportive way to reduce anxiety over time.

Homework: Practice assignments completed between CBT sessions that allow individuals to apply and strengthen skills learned in therapy in real-world situations.
